1. Quick Start

RetIQ runs entirely in your browser. There is no account to create and no data is sent to any server. To get started:

  1. Open retirementiq.app/app in any modern browser (Chrome, Safari, Edge, Firefox).
  2. Go to the Personal tab and enter your current age, retirement age, and birth year.
  3. Switch to Income and enter your current gross income and annual savings.
  4. In Expenses, enter your estimated annual spending.
  5. Under Accounts, add your investment accounts with current balances and types (Pre-Tax, Roth, Cash/HYS, or Brokerage).
  6. Click Dashboard in the top navigation to see your projection immediately.
Tip: RetIQ auto-saves to your browser's local storage. Your data persists between sessions automatically. Use Save/Load for backup or to transfer between devices.

2. Navigation & Layout

The app has two rows of navigation:

Top row (main nav): Switches between major views — Inputs, Dashboard, Projection, Monte Carlo, and Scenarios.

Sub-nav (inside Inputs): Switches between input categories — Personal, Income, Expenses, Accounts, Pension, Social Security, Disability (SSDI), Liabilities, Healthcare, Medicare, Roth Conversions, Charitable Giving, Legacy, and Assumptions. The sub-nav sticks to the top of the screen when you scroll.

The header bar also contains:

  • Essentials / Full Control mode toggle — switches between a simplified 7-tab view and the full 14-tab interface. See below.
  • Today's $ / Future $ toggle — switches all dollar values between inflation-adjusted and nominal.
  • Light / Dark theme toggle.
  • Save HTML / Load buttons for data portability.
  • Features / Manual links to documentation (opens on website).

Essentials vs. Full Control Mode

RetIQ has two UI modes that control which input tabs and fields are visible. The underlying engine always reads all parameters regardless of mode — switching modes never changes your projection numbers.

Essentials (default for new users): Shows 7 input tabs (Personal, Income, Expenses, Pension, Accounts, Social Security, Assumptions) with simplified fields. Advanced options like birth months, retirement months, COLA overrides, withdrawal order, and individual account management are hidden. The Accounts tab shows three aggregate balance fields (Pre-Tax, Roth, Brokerage) instead of individual accounts. Monte Carlo shows just a Run button without model selection.

Full Control (default for existing users): Shows all 14 input tabs and every advanced feature. This includes Disability (SSDI), Healthcare, Medicare, Roth Conversions, Charitable Giving, Legacy, and Liabilities tabs, plus full account management, withdrawal order configuration, and Monte Carlo model selection.

Toggle between modes anytime using the segmented button in the header. If you switch from Full Control to Essentials while advanced features are active, you'll see a confirmation listing what will be hidden. Your settings are never changed — switch back to Full Control to edit them.

Tip: A blue info bar appears in Essentials mode when advanced features are active in the background, showing how many and offering a quick switch to Full Control.

3. Personal Tab

Core demographic inputs that drive the entire projection.

FieldDescription
Current AgeYour age today. This is the starting point for the projection.
Retirement AgeAge you plan to stop working. Can be fractional (e.g., 62.5 for mid-year). Income stops and withdrawals begin at this age.
Plan End AgeAge at which the projection ends. Default 95. Set higher for conservative planning.
Birth YearUsed to determine FRA for Social Security and RMD start age.
Filing StatusTax filing status. Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) is used for household projections. Changes to Single after a spouse passes in survivor scenarios.

Spouse Section

Check "Include Spouse" to enable dual-household modeling. Enter the spouse's age, retirement age, birth year, and life expectancy independently. The spouse's income, savings, SS, pension, and SSDI all have their own inputs in their respective tabs.

Survivor Analysis

When spouse is enabled, you can set "Your Death Age" and "Spouse Death Age" to model what happens when one spouse passes. Expenses adjust by the survivor ratio, filing status changes, survivor SS benefits kick in, and life insurance death benefits (if configured in the Legacy tab) deposit tax-free into the survivor's portfolio.

4. Income Tab

FieldDescription
Annual Gross IncomeYour current pre-tax annual income. Grows by the income growth rate until retirement.
Annual SavingsHow much you save per year into investment accounts (401k, IRA, etc.). Grows by the savings growth rate.
Spouse Income / SavingsSame fields for spouse if enabled.
Income Growth RateAnnual raise percentage. Applied to both spouses. Typical: 2-5%.
Savings Growth RateAnnual increase in savings amount. Match income growth or set higher to model increasing savings rate.
Other Annual IncomeAdditional income (rental, part-time, annuities). Separate from pensions which have their own tab. Use Start Age / End Age to control when this income is active (defaults to retirement age through end of plan). Income is included in tax and MAGI calculations.

The tab shows a preview of projected income at retirement age based on current values and growth rates.

One-Time Cash Events (Full Control)

Model large one-time inflows such as selling a home or rental property. Each event specifies:

  • Age at Sale: When the event occurs in your projection.
  • Gross Amount: Sale price before costs.
  • Net After Selling Costs: Percentage remaining after agent fees, closing costs, repairs (e.g., 90%).
  • Cost Basis: Original purchase price plus improvements. Used to compute capital gains.
  • Property Type:Investment Property (full cap gains apply) or Primary Home (Section 121 exclusion: $250K single / $500K married filing jointly).
  • Deposit To: Brokerage (taxable) or Cash/HYS account.

Capital gains from cash events are taxed in the event year. The info boxes show your projected net proceeds and estimated capital gains.

Example — Home Downsizing: Selling a $600K primary home at age 72 with 90% net and $200K cost basis. For a married couple, the $340K net gain minus $500K exclusion = $0 taxable. Proceeds go to brokerage for continued growth.
Example — Rental Property: Selling a $400K rental at age 75 with 87% net and $150K basis. Net proceeds: $348K with $198K in taxable capital gains (no Section 121 exclusion for investment property).

5. Expenses Tab

RetIQ models expenses in two distinct phases: working years and retirement.

Pre-Retirement Expenses: Your annual living costs during working years — housing, food, transportation, childcare, insurance, and discretionary spending. These are deducted from your after-tax income alongside savings contributions and taxes. If your income can't cover expenses + savings + taxes, the shortfall is drawn from cash and brokerage accounts. A real-time cash flow breakdown shows your surplus or shortfall.

Pre-Retirement Expense Inflation: A separate inflation rate for your working-years costs (default 3%). This lets you model faster or slower cost growth independently of the general inflation rate used elsewhere.

Post-Retirement Expenses: Two modes:

Fixed Amount: Enter today's annual retirement expenses. They grow with the general inflation rate each year.

% of Portfolio: Withdraw a fixed percentage of your total portfolio each year in retirement (the "4% rule" approach).

Post-retirement expenses are further shaped by Expense Phases (Healthcare tab), pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs, and long-term care — all layered on top of the base amount.

Tip: Don't include savings contributions or taxes in your expense numbers — those are handled separately. Pre-retirement expenses often exceed post-retirement expenses due to commuting, work attire, childcare, and mortgage payments that may end before retirement.

6. Accounts Tab

Add each investment account with:

  • Name: Label for identification (e.g., "Vanguard 401k", "Fidelity Roth").
  • Type: Pre-Tax (traditional 401k/IRA), Roth (Roth 401k/IRA), Cash/HYS (savings, checking, CDs), or Brokerage (taxable investment accounts).
  • Balance: Current balance.

Cash/HYS and Brokerage are tracked as separate buckets with different tax treatment. Cash/HYS earns interest taxed as ordinary income, but withdrawals are tax-free (principal return). Brokerage withdrawals trigger capital gains at 50% estimated cost basis.

Contribution Limits

A checkbox at the bottom of the Accounts tab toggles contribution limit enforcement. When enabled, annual savings respect 401(k) limits ($23,500 in 2025), plus $7,500 catch-up at 50+, plus the enhanced $11,250 catch-up for ages 60–63 (SECURE Act 2.0). IRA limits ($7,000 + catch-up) also apply. Savings are allocated in order: pre-tax up to limit, then Roth up to limit, then overflow to brokerage.

In retirement, withdrawals follow a configurable 4-bucket order (default: Cash/HYS → Brokerage → Pre-Tax → Roth). Reorder in the Assumptions tab using the arrow buttons. RMDs are always taken from pre-tax regardless of withdrawal order.

7. Pension Tab

Model guaranteed income streams for either or both spouses:

  • Enable: Check to activate.
  • Name: Label (e.g., "Federal FERS", "Military Retirement").
  • Annual Amount: Starting annual benefit.
  • Start Age: When payments begin.
  • COLA: Per-pension annual cost-of-living adjustment. Set 0% for fixed pensions, 2-3% for COLA-adjusted. Each pension has its own independent COLA rate.
  • Survivor Benefit: What happens to the pension when the pensioner dies:
    • Single Life (no survivor): Highest monthly benefit, but stops completely at death. No income for surviving spouse.
    • Joint & 50% Survivor: Monthly benefit is reduced ~5-10%, but surviving spouse receives 50% of the pension for life.
    • Joint & 75% Survivor: Larger monthly reduction, surviving spouse receives 75%.
    • Joint & 100% Survivor: Largest reduction (~10-20%), but full pension continues for the surviving spouse.
  • Taxable Percentage: The portion of pension income subject to federal/state tax. Most pensions are 100% taxable. Set lower for:
    • Military disability pensions — may be partially or fully tax-exempt.
    • After-tax contributions — the IRS Simplified Method determines the tax-free portion.

Pension income flows into the projection as cash received; only the taxable portion (pension × taxable%) is included in taxable income for federal and state tax calculations.

Plan Health: If a pension is set to Single Life and you have a spouse, Plan Health will flag the survivor risk and suggest reviewing Joint & Survivor options.

8. Social Security Tab

Claim Age

Set the age you plan to start collecting SS (62-70). Earlier means smaller monthly checks; later means larger. The comparison table below shows monthly and annual amounts at each claiming age, the percentage of FRA benefit, and the breakeven age vs. claiming at 62.

Benefit Estimation

Two methods:

Income-based estimate: Enter your average annual income and RetIQ estimates your PIA using 2025 SSA bend points ($1,226 / $7,391).

Manual SSA entry: Check "Use your SSA statement amount" and enter the monthly benefit at FRA shown on your Social Security statement (ssa.gov). RetIQ then applies the exact SSA early/late adjustment to that amount.

Tip: For the most accurate results, log into ssa.gov, find your "Estimated Benefits" statement, and enter the monthly amount shown at your Full Retirement Age. RetIQ will handle the rest.

Survivor Benefits

Set a Survivor SS Claim Age for when the surviving spouse would begin collecting the deceased spouse's benefit. The higher of the survivor's own benefit or the deceased's benefit is used (per SSA rules).

Retirement Earnings Test

If you claim Social Security before your Full Retirement Age and continue working, SSA reduces your benefit based on earned income. RetIQ models this automatically:

  • Under FRA (all year): $1 withheld for every $2 earned above $23,400 (2025 limit).
  • FRA year: $1 withheld for every $3 earned above $62,160 (2025 limit), using full annual earnings.
  • At or after FRA: No withholding — the earnings test does not apply.

The limits are indexed to average wage growth (~3%/yr). Each spouse’s earnings test is applied independently. SSDI recipients are exempt — SSDI uses separate Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) rules instead.

When withholding applies, the projection table shows a SS W/H column (red) with the amount withheld each year. The This Year’s Plan card annotates the Social Security row with the withheld amount. The SS income shown everywhere is net of withholding.

Note: Benefits withheld before FRA are not lost — SSA recalculates your monthly benefit upward at FRA to credit the withheld months. RetIQ does not yet model this FRA recalculation (planned for a future version), so the projection is slightly conservative for early claimers who have benefits withheld.

SSA Calculation Precision

RetIQ matches SSA calculations exactly: FRA is computed in integer months per birth year, early reduction uses the exact 5/9% (first 36 months) and 5/12% (beyond 36 months) rates, delayed credits use the exact 2/3% per month rate, and the final benefit is rounded down to the whole dollar per SSA rules.

9. Disability (SSDI) Tab

Social Security Disability Insurance replaces a portion of your income if you become unable to work due to a qualifying disability. RetIQ models SSDI for either or both spouses, including its downstream effects on Medicare eligibility, Social Security transitions, taxes, and survivor benefits.

Setting Up SSDI

Enable SSDI for yourself, your spouse, or both. Each person's SSDI is tracked independently. For each recipient, enter:

  • Monthly SSDI Amount: The benefit amount from your SSA award letter. If you haven't applied yet, use your SSA statement's disability estimate. This amount is essentially your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA).
  • Start Age: The age you begin receiving SSDI payments. Keep in mind that SSA has a five-month waiting period after your disability onset before benefits begin, so your start age should reflect when payments actually begin — not the disability onset itself.
  • End Age (converts to SS): Typically your Full Retirement Age (FRA). At FRA, SSDI automatically converts to regular Social Security retirement benefits at the same dollar amount. You don't need to do anything — SSA handles the conversion.

When you enable SSDI, RetIQ displays an info box showing the annualized income, plus a claiming-age comparison table showing what your regular SS benefit would be at each age from 62 to 70. This helps you understand what happens after SSDI ends.

How RetIQ Models the SSDI → SS Transition

During the SSDI active period, RetIQ includes your SSDI payment as income. Regular SS benefits are suppressed — you can't collect both simultaneously. Once SSDI ends (at the End Age you specify), your regular SS claiming-age logic from the Social Security tab takes over.

In most cases, set the SSDI End Age equal to your FRA and the SS Claim Age in the SS tab to the same value. This models the real-world automatic conversion. However, if you want to explore what happens if you delay your SS claim past FRA (to earn delayed retirement credits), you can set a later SS Claim Age — but be aware that SSA will not let you delay past FRA when converting from SSDI. RetIQ does not enforce this rule for you, so be careful with this setting.

Medicare Eligibility: The 24-Month Rule

One of the most important financial effects of SSDI is early Medicare eligibility. Under federal law, SSDI recipients qualify for Medicare after receiving disability benefits for 24 months. Combined with the 5-month SSDI waiting period, most people become Medicare-eligible about 29 months after disability onset.

RetIQ automatically calculates your effective Medicare start age as SSDI Start Age + 2 years (or age 65, whichever comes first). This flows through to three areas of the projection:

  • Pre-Medicare healthcare gap: If you have pre-Medicare healthcare costs enabled in the Healthcare tab, those costs will end at your early Medicare age instead of 65. For someone who starts SSDI at age 50, this eliminates 13 years of gap coverage costs.
  • Medicare premiums and IRMAA: Part B and Part D premiums (including any IRMAA surcharges) begin at your effective Medicare age, not 65.
  • Medicare Dashboard: The lifetime Medicare cost summary reflects the earlier start, and the chart label updates to show the actual age (e.g., "from 52" instead of "from 65").

Two exceptions exist in real life: recipients with ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease) receive Medicare immediately with no waiting period, and those with End-Stage Renal Disease also qualify immediately. RetIQ does not have special handling for these — to approximate, set the SSDI start age such that Start Age + 2 equals your desired Medicare start.

Taxes on SSDI

SSDI is taxed identically to regular Social Security benefits. Up to 85% of your SSDI income may be taxable, depending on your total combined income (the same provisional-income formula used for SS). RetIQ applies this automatically — you do not need to configure anything differently from regular SS taxation.

Survivor Benefits When an SSDI Recipient Dies

If you're modeling survivor scenarios (in the Personal tab), SSDI interacts with survivor benefits in important ways:

  • SSDI stops at death. If the SSDI recipient passes away, their disability payments end immediately. The surviving spouse does not inherit the SSDI payment itself.
  • Survivor SS benefit from the deceased's record. The surviving spouse can claim a survivor benefit based on the deceased's Social Security work record. RetIQ uses the deceased's PIA to calculate this. If the deceased was on SSDI but had no separate SS income entered, RetIQ uses the SSDI monthly amount as the PIA (since SSDI is calculated from the same work record).
  • The higher-of rule. The surviving spouse receives the higher of their own SS benefit or the survivor benefit — not both. Before the survivor's own SS claim age, they receive just the survivor benefit as a bridge. At their own claim age, RetIQ takes the maximum of the two.
  • Survivor is also on SSDI. If the surviving spouse is also on SSDI, SSA pays the difference between the survivor benefit and the SSDI amount (if the survivor benefit is higher). RetIQ models this correctly — SSDI income continues, and any excess survivor benefit is added on top.

Common Scenarios

Scenario 1: Early disability, long SSDI period. Person becomes disabled at 45, starts SSDI at 45. Medicare kicks in at 47 (eliminating 18 years of pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs). SSDI converts to SS at FRA (67). Set Start Age = 45, End Age = 67, SS Claim Age = 67.

Scenario 2: Late-career disability. Person becomes disabled at 58, starts SSDI at 58. Medicare kicks in at 60 (5 years earlier than normal). SSDI converts to SS at 67. Set Start Age = 58, End Age = 67, SS Claim Age = 67.

Scenario 3: Spouse on SSDI with survivor planning. Primary earner is healthy; spouse is on SSDI. Configure SSDI for the spouse, then use the Survivor section in the Personal tab. If the spouse passes, their SSDI stops and the primary loses that household income. If the primary passes, the spouse on SSDI may be able to receive a survivor benefit on top of their SSDI (the difference, if the primary's record is larger).

Where to find your SSDI amount: Your benefit letter from SSA states your monthly disability amount. If you haven't applied yet, log in to my Social Security and check the disability estimate on your statement. This is an estimate based on your current earnings record.

10. Healthcare Tab

If you retire before Medicare eligibility, you need to bridge the healthcare gap. RetIQ lets you choose from six coverage sources, each with its own cost defaults and engine behavior:

Coverage Sources

  • ACA Marketplace: Individual or family plan from Healthcare.gov. RetIQ dynamically calculates your ACA premium tax credit (subsidy) based on your projected MAGI each year. See the ACA Subsidy Engine section below for details.
  • Employer Retiree Plan: Subsidized coverage from your former employer. Default ~$6K single / ~$12K couple. Typically the cheapest option.
  • COBRA Continuation: Continue your employer plan up to 18 months (36 for qualifying disability). You pay the full premium plus 2% admin. The projection models a two-phase cost: COBRA cost during the COBRA period, then an automatic switch to a follow-up cost (typically ACA rates) for the remaining gap.
  • Spouse’s Employer Plan: Coverage under your spouse’s active employer plan. Ends when your spouse retires, then switches to a follow-up cost for the remaining gap.
  • VA Healthcare: Veterans with qualifying service may have zero or very low healthcare costs. Modeled as $0.
  • Other / Self-Pay: Health sharing ministries, short-term plans, direct primary care, or self-pay arrangements.

Fields

  • Annual Cost (Year 1): Base annual cost for your selected source. Defaults adjust for single vs. couple based on whether a spouse is enabled.
  • Healthcare Inflation: Annual increase in healthcare costs. Default: 4–5% depending on source (healthcare historically inflates faster than general CPI).
  • COBRA Duration: (COBRA only) Months of COBRA coverage before follow-up kicks in. Default: 18.
  • Follow-Up Annual Cost: (COBRA and Spouse Plan only) Cost after the primary source ends. Defaults to ACA marketplace rates.

The tab shows a cumulative healthcare cost summary for the gap years, annotated with the source phasing (e.g., “COBRA 18mo then follow-up”).

Smart Defaults

When you switch sources, RetIQ updates cost and inflation fields only if they still match the previous source’s defaults. If you’ve customized a value, it’s preserved.

ACA Subsidy Engine

When you select ACA Marketplace, RetIQ replaces the flat annual cost with a dynamic premium calculation based on your projected income (MAGI) each year. This is how the real ACA works — your premium depends on your income relative to the Federal Poverty Level (FPL).

How it works each year:

  1. RetIQ computes your projected MAGI (earned income, Social Security, pension, taxable withdrawals, Roth conversions, capital gains).
  2. MAGI is compared to the Federal Poverty Level for your household size to determine your FPL percentage.
  3. An “applicable percentage” is looked up from IRS tables — this is the share of income you’re expected to pay toward your premium.
  4. Your expected contribution = MAGI × applicable percentage.
  5. Your subsidy = benchmark premium − expected contribution (minimum $0).
  6. Your net premium = benchmark premium − subsidy (this is what you actually pay).

ACA-specific fields:

  • Household Size: You + spouse + dependents enrolled on the ACA plan. Defaults to 2 if a spouse is enabled, otherwise 1.
  • Benchmark Premium Override: Enter your actual Second Lowest Cost Silver Plan (SLCSP) annual premium from healthcare.gov for a more accurate projection. Leave blank and RetIQ uses national-average estimates adjusted for age and household size.
  • ACA Subsidy Rules: Choose Auto (selects by projection year), Enhanced (2021–2025 ARPA/IRA rules with no income cliff), or Original (standard ACA with a hard subsidy cliff at 400% FPL). Enhanced subsidies expired after 2025; select Enhanced if Congress extends them.

Year-1 Subsidy Preview: The UI shows a live preview of your estimated subsidy at retirement based on current inputs — FPL percentage, benchmark premium, estimated subsidy, and net premium. The actual projection uses MAGI computed year by year, which may differ from this preview.

Income management tip: During ACA years, every source of income affects your premium. Pre-tax withdrawals, Roth conversions, Social Security, capital gains, and pension income all count toward MAGI. Roth account withdrawals do not increase MAGI and will not affect your ACA subsidy. This is one reason some retirees prioritize Roth withdrawals during their ACA gap years.
Roth conversion trade-off: Roth conversions during ACA years increase your MAGI, which can reduce or eliminate your ACA subsidy. The Conversion Impact Analysis on the Roth Conversion tab shows the ACA Subsidy Delta — how much subsidy you lose (or keep) compared to not converting. Factor this hidden cost into your conversion strategy.

SSDI and early Medicare: If you have SSDI enabled, RetIQ automatically shortens the healthcare gap. SSDI recipients qualify for Medicare after 24 months of benefits, so your gap coverage ends at your SSDI Start Age + 2 (or 65, whichever is earlier). See the Disability (SSDI) Tab section for details.

Long-Term Care (LTC) Insurance Inflation

The Healthcare tab includes LTC modeling. When you enable LTC insurance, two additional fields control how your insurance benefit grows over time:

  • Insurance Inflation Protection: Most LTC policies include inflation protection riders that increase benefits annually. Options: None (0%), 2% Compound, 3% Compound (most common), or 5% Compound. Without this setting, the model would subtract a flat benefit from inflating care costs, dramatically overstating your out-of-pocket expenses.
  • Policy Purchase Age: The age when you bought the policy. Benefits compound from this age forward, so a policy purchased at 55 with 3% inflation will have a much larger benefit at age 82 than one purchased at 70.

The info boxes show your projected care cost, insurance coverage, and gap at the LTC start age so you can see whether your policy keeps pace with rising care costs.

2025 LTC cost benchmarks: Nursing home: $110–130K/year. Assisted living: $70–75K/year. Home health aide: $75–80K/year (national medians). LTC costs inflate at roughly 5% annually.

11. Medicare Tab

Medicare modeling begins at age 65 for most people, or earlier if SSDI triggers the 24-month early eligibility rule (see Disability (SSDI) Tab). Key components:

  • Base Part B premium: $185/month (2025).
  • IRMAA surcharges: Additional premiums triggered when your MAGI (from 2 years prior) exceeds income thresholds. Roth conversions count toward MAGI and can trigger IRMAA.
  • Medical inflation: Medicare costs grow at 5% annually (configurable).

The Medicare tab shows a year-by-year breakdown of premiums, IRMAA surcharges, and total Medicare costs. This feeds into the projection as a healthcare expense line.

Note: Roth conversions increase your MAGI and can push you into higher IRMAA brackets. The Roth Conversion Optimizer accounts for this when scoring strategies.

12. Roth Conversion Tab

This tab has two sections that work together: the Strategy (your active plan) and the Optimizer (a research tool). Understanding the relationship between them is key.

Roth Conversion Strategy (your active plan)

Whatever you configure here is your live conversion plan — it feeds directly into the Dashboard, Projection table, Monte Carlo, and Scenarios. Toggle the checkbox to enable or disable conversions entirely.

Six strategy types:

  • Fixed Amount: Convert a set dollar amount each year (e.g., $50K/yr).
  • Fill Tax Bracket: Convert enough each year to fill taxable income up to a target bracket (e.g., fill the 22% bracket). This is the most common approach — it automatically adjusts the conversion amount based on your other income in each year.
  • Smart Fill (IRMAA-Aware): Like Fill Tax Bracket, but caps conversions before crossing the next Medicare IRMAA tier. Prevents surprise surcharges that can erase conversion benefits.
  • Target Income (MAGI) Ceiling: Convert up to a total Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) limit. Useful for staying below Medicare surcharge (IRMAA) thresholds.
  • % of Pre-Tax Balance: Convert a percentage of remaining pre-tax balance each year. The amount naturally decreases as the balance shrinks.
  • Custom Schedule: Set a specific conversion amount for each year in the conversion window. Use the Generate Optimal button to auto-fill amounts based on greedy per-year optimization.

Start / End Age defines the conversion window. The sweet spot is typically between retirement (when earned income drops) and RMD start age (when forced distributions begin).

Impact Analysis

Directly below the strategy inputs, this section compares your current strategy against doing no conversions at all. It shows total converted, tax cost, lifetime tax delta, RMD reduction, IRMAA impact, final net worth change, and breakeven age.

Four charts visualize the strategy:

  • Conversion Advantage: A single line showing the tax-adjusted net worth difference between converting and not converting. Above zero means conversions are winning; below zero means you’re still recouping the upfront tax cost. The zero-crossing is the breakeven age.
  • Pre-Tax Balance: Shows the drawdown of pre-tax accounts (lower = less future RMDs).
  • Roth Balance: Shows the growth of tax-free Roth assets.
  • Annual Conversions & Tax: Bar-style chart of each year’s conversion amount alongside its tax cost.

This section updates instantly whenever you change any strategy parameter.

Roth Conversion Optimizer (research tool)

Click Find Optimal Strategy to test ~200+ combinations of fixed amounts ($5K–$200K), fill-bracket targets (12%/22%/24%), Smart Fill targets (IRMAA-aware bracket fill), and age windows. The optimizer ranks the top 8 results by a composite score that weighs tax savings, IRMAA impact, and net worth gain.

The optimizer does not change your plan automatically. It shows you what could work better and lets you decide.

Click Apply on any row to load that strategy into your active plan. The Strategy section updates with the new settings, the Impact Analysis re-computes, and all other tabs (Dashboard, Projection, Monte Carlo) reflect the change. The optimizer table stays visible with a “✓ Applied” marker so you can compare.

Typical workflow: (1) Set a reasonable starting strategy (e.g., Fill 22%, ages 65–72). (2) Review the Impact Analysis. (3) Run the Optimizer to see if there’s something better. (4) Apply a result or adjust manually. (5) Check the Dashboard and Monte Carlo to confirm the strategy works across scenarios. Note: the optimizer tests fixed-amount, fill-bracket, and Smart Fill strategies. For Target MAGI, % of Balance, or Custom Schedule, configure those manually.

13. Charity / QCD Tab

  • Annual Charitable Giving: Total planned charitable donations per year.
  • Use QCD: Enable Qualified Charitable Distributions from IRAs. QCDs satisfy RMDs without being included in taxable income. 2025 limit: $108,000/year.

If QCD is enabled, charitable giving is sourced from RMD amounts first, reducing taxable income. Any giving beyond RMDs is treated as a standard charitable deduction.

14. Legacy Tab

Set a target inheritance amount (today's dollars). The dashboard and projection track whether your plan achieves this goal at end-of-plan. The legacy target is inflation-adjusted in projections.

Legacy Composition Breakdown

Below the legacy goal inputs, a detailed composition analysis shows what your heirs actually inherit and the tax implications:

  • Stacked bar: Visual split of your end-of-plan net worth by account type — Pre-Tax (blue), Roth (cyan), and Taxable (amber).
  • Per-account detail: Each account type shows its ending balance, percentage of total, and heir tax treatment:
    • Pre-Tax (401k/IRA): Subject to SECURE Act 10-year distribution rule — heirs must withdraw and pay income tax within 10 years.
    • Roth: Tax-free to heirs (10-year rule applies but no tax on withdrawals).
    • Taxable (Brokerage): Receives stepped-up cost basis at death — only post-inheritance gains are taxed.
  • After-tax legacy estimate: Applies an assumed 22% heir tax bracket to the pre-tax portion to show the approximate after-tax inheritance.

Roth Conversion Legacy Comparison

If Roth conversions are enabled in your plan, the composition section automatically runs a second projection with conversions disabled. The comparison shows how much more (or less) your heirs inherit in after-tax terms thanks to your Roth conversion strategy. This helps quantify the multi-generational benefit of paying taxes now so heirs receive tax-free Roth assets instead of taxable pre-tax accounts.

Tip: If the composition shows your legacy is mostly pre-tax, consider Roth conversions — even modest conversions shift the inheritance toward tax-free Roth, reducing your heirs' 10-year tax burden under the SECURE Act.

When life insurance policies are configured (see Life Insurance below), death benefits received during the projection increase the taxable account balance. The composition breakdown annotates this with the total insurance contribution. Since death benefits are tax-free, they improve the after-tax legacy without adding to the pre-tax burden on heirs.

Life Insurance

Life insurance policies are configured in the Legacy tab (Full Control mode). Click "+ Add Life Insurance Policy" to add a new policy. Each policy has:

  • Insured Person: You or your spouse. Determines when premiums stop and benefits trigger.
  • Policy Type: Term (expires at a set age) or Permanent (whole life/universal — active until death).
  • Death Benefit: The tax-free lump sum paid to beneficiaries at death.
  • Annual Premium: Level annual cost added to your expenses. Not inflation-adjusted.
  • Expires at Age (term only): Coverage and premiums end at this age. No payout if the insured is alive.
  • Beneficiary: Surviving spouse (default) or estate.

How It Works in the Projection

  • Premiums: Added to annual expenses each year the policy is active. For term policies, premiums stop at the expiration age. For permanent policies, premiums continue until death.
  • Death Benefit: When the insured person dies (set via Survivor Analysis in the Personal tab), the death benefit is deposited tax-free into the brokerage account. It is not counted as taxable income — it directly increases the survivor's portfolio balance.
  • Legacy Impact: Death benefits appear in the Legacy Composition breakdown, increasing the taxable (brokerage) account balance with an annotation showing the insurance contribution.

Key Notes

  • Death benefits are tax-free under IRC §101. They don't affect MAGI, IRMAA, ACA subsidies, or any income-based calculations.
  • To see death benefit impacts, you must configure death ages in the Personal tab (Survivor Analysis).
  • If a term policy expires before the modeled death age, it provides no benefit — only premiums were paid. The strategy report flags this scenario.
  • Cash value modeling for permanent policies (borrowing, surrendering) is not currently supported.

Retirement Withdrawal Order

This is the order RetIQ draws from your accounts in retirement. The default order (Cash → Brokerage → Pre-Tax → Roth) is a widely-used approach: spend taxable money first and let tax-advantaged accounts keep growing. Roth goes last because it grows tax-free and passes to heirs tax-free. Use the arrow buttons to reorder, or click “Reset to recommended” to restore the default. Each bucket has different tax treatment:

  • Cash / HYS: Zero capital gains — pure principal return. Interest is taxed annually as ordinary income regardless of withdrawals.
  • Brokerage: Gains taxed at capital gains rate (estimated 50% cost basis).
  • Pre-Tax (401k/IRA): Fully taxable as ordinary income.
  • Roth: Tax-free withdrawals.

RMDs are always taken from pre-tax accounts regardless of this order. The withdrawal order only affects discretionary spending above RMDs.

Tip: Drawing Cash/HYS first preserves tax-advantaged growth in Roth and pre-tax accounts. Experiment with different orders to see the tax impact in the Projection table.

19. Monte Carlo Simulation

The Monte Carlo engine runs randomized projections to estimate the probability your money lasts through your plan. Choose from three simulation models, each offering a different perspective on your plan’s resilience.

Simulation Models

  • Normal (Bell Curve): The standard approach. Each year’s return is drawn independently from a symmetric bell curve (Gaussian distribution) centered on your expected return. This is the gentlest model — it produces realistic year-to-year variation but underweights extreme events like major crashes. Use this as your baseline.
  • Historical (1928–2024): Each simulation randomly reshuffles actual US stock market returns and inflation from 97 years of history. Returns and inflation are paired — a year with poor returns and high inflation (like 1974) stays paired together. This naturally captures real crash magnitudes (the Great Depression, 1970s stagflation, 2008), fat tails, and the correlation between returns and inflation. No standard deviation settings are needed — volatility comes from the data itself.
  • Stress Test (Fat-Tailed): Like Normal but uses a heavy-tailed statistical distribution (Student’s t) that produces extreme events — both crashes and booms — more frequently than a bell curve. If your plan survives this model, it has significant resilience. Use this when you want to test against worse-than-historical scenarios.

Configuration

  • Number of Simulations: Default 500. Higher means more stable results but slower.
  • Return Std Dev: Year-to-year volatility of returns (Normal and Stress Test models only). Default: 6%.
  • Inflation Std Dev: Year-to-year inflation volatility (Normal and Stress Test models only). Default: 1.5%.

Results

  • Success Rate: Percentage of simulations where money lasts through end-of-plan.
  • Percentile Chart: Shows 10th, 50th (median), and 90th percentile net worth trajectories.
  • Average Bust Age: Among failed simulations, the average age when money runs out.
Pro tip: Run all three models and compare. If all three show 90%+ success, your plan is robust. If Normal shows 92% but Historical shows 78% and Stress Test shows 65%, your plan works in gentle conditions but is vulnerable to real-world market patterns — consider reducing expenses, delaying retirement, or increasing savings.
Interpretation: A 90%+ success rate is generally considered strong. Below 80% suggests your plan may need adjustment. Historical and Stress Test models are inherently more demanding than Normal — a lower success rate in these models is expected and informative, not necessarily alarming.

24. FAQ

Is my data secure?

Yes. RetIQ runs entirely in your browser. No financial data is ever sent to any server. The only network calls are for license verification (license key only) and optional PWA updates. You can verify this yourself using View Page Source or browser DevTools. For a complete audit of every network request, storage key, and privacy measure, see the Privacy & Security Transparency page.

Which tax brackets does RetIQ use?

2025 federal brackets (Married Filing Jointly and single) as updated by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. State taxes use progressive brackets for bracketed states (e.g. California, New York, New Jersey) and flat rates where applicable (e.g. Illinois 4.95%, Pennsylvania 3.07%). Social Security exemptions with age and income gates, retirement income exclusions for pensions and IRA/401k distributions, and filing-status-aware schedules are applied automatically for each state. All 50 states + DC are available in the Assumptions tab dropdown.

How accurate are the Social Security estimates?

RetIQ matches SSA calculations exactly when using the manual entry mode (entering your FRA benefit from ssa.gov). The income-based estimate uses 2025 bend points and is a good approximation but may differ from SSA's indexed earnings calculation.

Does RetIQ account for Required Minimum Distributions?

Yes. RMDs are computed per the Uniform Lifetime Table with SECURE Act 2.0 start ages. They are sourced from pre-tax accounts and included in taxable income. QCDs can offset RMDs if enabled.

Can I model my spouse dying before me?

Yes. Set "Spouse Death Age" in the Personal tab. The projection will switch to single filing status, adjust expenses by the survivor ratio, and calculate survivor SS benefits from that age forward.

What does the Monte Carlo success rate mean?

It is the percentage of randomized simulations where your money lasts through end-of-plan. A 95% success rate means that in 950 out of 1,000 random market scenarios, your plan survived. Generally, 90%+ is considered strong.

Can I use RetIQ on my phone?

Yes. RetIQ is fully responsive. Install it as a PWA on iOS or Android for the best mobile experience.

What is the difference between Cash/HYS and Brokerage?

Cash/HYS accounts (high-yield savings, CDs, checking) earn interest taxed as ordinary income each year, but withdrawals are tax-free because you are withdrawing principal. Brokerage accounts hold investments whose withdrawals trigger capital gains taxes on the appreciated portion (modeled at 50% cost basis). RetIQ tracks them as separate buckets with independent return rates, withdrawal priorities, and tax treatment.

How do I reset all my data?

Clear your browser's local storage for retirementiq.app, or use your browser's developer tools (Application > Local Storage > Clear). A fresh reload will start with default values.

Does SSDI affect my Medicare start age?

Yes. RetIQ automatically starts Medicare at SSDI Start Age + 2 years (or 65, whichever is first). This reflects the federal 24-month qualifying period. Pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs, Medicare premiums, and IRMAA all adjust accordingly. See the Disability (SSDI) Tab section for full details and scenarios.

How do I find the best strategy for my situation?

Use the Strategy Report Card on the Dashboard. It automatically tests alternative SS timing, Roth conversion, and retirement age strategies against your current plan. Choose your goal (max net worth, min taxes, etc.) and the table ranks every alternative. Click Apply on any row to adopt it instantly. For deeper Roth analysis, use the Roth Optimizer. For stress-testing, use Scenario Comparison and Monte Carlo.

How can I verify the calculations are correct?

Open the Validation tab in the app's main navigation. It displays 254 automated tests grouped by category, each showing the expected value (from IRS/SSA/CMS sources), the engine's actual result, and a pass/fail status. You can also right-click the app and select View Page Source to inspect the calculation code directly. See the Validation Report section for details on sources and methodology.

What happens to my spouse's benefits if I'm on SSDI and die?

Your SSDI payments stop, but your surviving spouse can claim a survivor benefit based on your Social Security work record. RetIQ calculates this from your PIA (or your SSDI amount if no separate SS income was entered). The survivor receives the higher of their own SS benefit or the survivor benefit — not both.
