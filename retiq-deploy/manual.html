1. Quick Start

RetIQ runs entirely in your browser. There is no account to create and no data is sent to any server. To get started:

  1. Open retiqstudio.com/app in any modern browser (Chrome, Safari, Edge, Firefox).
  2. Go to the Personal tab and enter your current age, retirement age, and birth year.
  3. Switch to Income and enter your current gross income and annual savings.
  4. In Expenses, enter your estimated annual spending.
  5. Under Accounts, add your investment accounts with current balances and types (Pre-Tax, Roth, Taxable).
  6. Click Dashboard in the top navigation to see your projection immediately.
Tip: RetIQ auto-saves to your browser's local storage. Your data persists between sessions automatically. Use Save/Load for backup or to transfer between devices.

2. Navigation & Layout

The app has two rows of navigation:

Top row (main nav): Switches between major views — Inputs, Dashboard, Projection, Monte Carlo, and Scenarios.

Sub-nav (inside Inputs): Switches between input categories — Personal, Income, Expenses, Accounts, Pension, Social Security, SSDI, Liabilities, Healthcare, Medicare, Roth Conv., Charity/QCD, Legacy, and Assumptions. The sub-nav sticks to the top of the screen when you scroll.

The header bar also contains:

  • Today's $ / Future $ toggle — switches all dollar values between inflation-adjusted and nominal.
  • Light / Dark theme toggle.
  • Save HTML / Load buttons for data portability.
  • Features / Manual links to documentation (opens on website).

5. Expenses Tab

RetIQ models expenses in two distinct phases: working years and retirement.

Pre-Retirement Expenses: Your annual living costs during working years — housing, food, transportation, childcare, insurance, and discretionary spending. These are deducted from your after-tax income alongside savings contributions and taxes. If your income can't cover expenses + savings + taxes, the shortfall is drawn from taxable accounts. A real-time cash flow breakdown shows your surplus or shortfall.

Pre-Retirement Expense Inflation: A separate inflation rate for your working-years costs (default 3%). This lets you model faster or slower cost growth independently of the general inflation rate used elsewhere.

Post-Retirement Expenses: Two modes:

Fixed Amount: Enter today's annual retirement expenses. They grow with the general inflation rate each year.

% of Portfolio: Withdraw a fixed percentage of your total portfolio each year in retirement (the "4% rule" approach).

Post-retirement expenses are further shaped by Expense Phases (Healthcare tab), pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs, and long-term care — all layered on top of the base amount.

Tip: Don't include savings contributions or taxes in your expense numbers — those are handled separately. Pre-retirement expenses often exceed post-retirement expenses due to commuting, work attire, childcare, and mortgage payments that may end before retirement.

6. Accounts Tab

Add each investment account with:

  • Name: Label for identification (e.g., "Vanguard 401k", "Fidelity Roth").
  • Type: Pre-Tax (traditional 401k/IRA), Roth (Roth 401k/IRA), or Taxable (brokerage).
  • Balance: Current balance.
  • Return Rate: Expected nominal annual return for this specific account. Defaults to 7%. This lets you model different growth rates (e.g., 4% for a HYSA, 7% for a stock fund, 10% for an aggressive growth fund). The Monte Carlo engine scales volatility proportionally to each account's return rate.

RetIQ enforces annual contribution limits: $23,500 for 401k (2025), plus $7,500 catch-up at 50+, plus the enhanced $11,250 catch-up for ages 60-63. Savings are allocated to accounts in order: pre-tax up to limit, then Roth up to limit, then overflow to taxable.

In retirement, withdrawals come from taxable first, then pre-tax (including RMDs), then Roth last — the tax-efficient ordering.

7. Pension Tab

Model guaranteed income streams for either or both spouses:

  • Enable: Check to activate.
  • Name: Label (e.g., "Federal FERS", "Military Retirement").
  • Annual Amount: Starting annual benefit.
  • Start Age: When payments begin.
  • COLA: Annual cost-of-living adjustment, shared between both pensions. Set 0% for fixed pensions, 2-3% for COLA-adjusted.

Pension income is included in taxable income each year it is received.

8. Social Security Tab

Claim Age

Set the age you plan to start collecting SS (62-70). Earlier means smaller monthly checks; later means larger. The comparison table below shows monthly and annual amounts at each claiming age, the percentage of FRA benefit, and the breakeven age vs. claiming at 62.

Benefit Estimation

Two methods:

Income-based estimate: Enter your average annual income and RetIQ estimates your PIA using 2025 SSA bend points ($1,226 / $7,391).

Manual SSA entry: Check "Use your SSA statement amount" and enter the monthly benefit at FRA shown on your Social Security statement (ssa.gov). RetIQ then applies the exact SSA early/late adjustment to that amount.

Tip: For the most accurate results, log into ssa.gov, find your "Estimated Benefits" statement, and enter the monthly amount shown at your Full Retirement Age. RetIQ will handle the rest.

Survivor Benefits

Set a Survivor SS Claim Age for when the surviving spouse would begin collecting the deceased spouse's benefit. The higher of the survivor's own benefit or the deceased's benefit is used (per SSA rules).

SSA Calculation Precision

RetIQ matches SSA calculations exactly: FRA is computed in integer months per birth year, early reduction uses the exact 5/9% (first 36 months) and 5/12% (beyond 36 months) rates, delayed credits use the exact 2/3% per month rate, and the final benefit is rounded down to the whole dollar per SSA rules.

9. SSDI Tab

Social Security Disability Insurance replaces a portion of your income if you become unable to work due to a qualifying disability. RetIQ models SSDI for either or both spouses, including its downstream effects on Medicare eligibility, Social Security transitions, taxes, and survivor benefits.

Setting Up SSDI

Enable SSDI for yourself, your spouse, or both. Each person's SSDI is tracked independently. For each recipient, enter:

  • Monthly SSDI Amount: The benefit amount from your SSA award letter. If you haven't applied yet, use your SSA statement's disability estimate. This amount is essentially your Primary Insurance Amount (PIA).
  • Start Age: The age you begin receiving SSDI payments. Keep in mind that SSA has a five-month waiting period after your disability onset before benefits begin, so your start age should reflect when payments actually begin — not the disability onset itself.
  • End Age (converts to SS): Typically your Full Retirement Age (FRA). At FRA, SSDI automatically converts to regular Social Security retirement benefits at the same dollar amount. You don't need to do anything — SSA handles the conversion.

When you enable SSDI, RetIQ displays an info box showing the annualized income, plus an SSDI → SS Transition table showing the SSDI active period, the auto-conversion age and FRA benefit amount, and your Medicare eligibility date. If your SS claim age setting conflicts with the auto-conversion, a warning is displayed.

How RetIQ Models the SSDI → SS Transition

During the SSDI active period, RetIQ includes your SSDI payment as income. Regular SS benefits are suppressed — you can't collect both simultaneously. When SSDI ends (at the End Age you specify), RetIQ automatically converts to regular SS at FRA. The SS Claim Age you set in the Social Security tab is overridden for SSDI recipients — SSA does not allow delaying past FRA when converting from SSDI. RetIQ enforces this rule: the effective claim age is always End Age + 1 (typically FRA), and the benefit is calculated at the FRA amount with no delayed credits.

Both the Social Security tab and the SSDI tab display banners when SSDI is active, clearly indicating which claim age setting is being used in the projection.

Medicare Eligibility: The 24-Month Rule

One of the most important financial effects of SSDI is early Medicare eligibility. Under federal law, SSDI recipients qualify for Medicare after receiving disability benefits for 24 months. Combined with the 5-month SSDI waiting period, most people become Medicare-eligible about 29 months after disability onset.

RetIQ automatically calculates your effective Medicare start age as SSDI Start Age + 2 years (or age 65, whichever comes first). This flows through to three areas of the projection:

  • Pre-Medicare healthcare gap: If you have pre-Medicare healthcare costs enabled in the Healthcare tab, those costs will end at your early Medicare age instead of 65. For someone who starts SSDI at age 50, this eliminates 13 years of gap coverage costs.
  • Medicare premiums and IRMAA: Part B and Part D premiums (including any IRMAA surcharges) begin at your effective Medicare age, not 65.
  • Medicare Dashboard: The lifetime Medicare cost summary reflects the earlier start, and the chart label updates to show the actual age (e.g., "from 52" instead of "from 65").

Two exceptions exist in real life: recipients with ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease) receive Medicare immediately with no waiting period, and those with End-Stage Renal Disease also qualify immediately. RetIQ does not have special handling for these — to approximate, set the SSDI start age such that Start Age + 2 equals your desired Medicare start.

Taxes on SSDI

SSDI is taxed identically to regular Social Security benefits. Up to 85% of your SSDI income may be taxable, depending on your total combined income (the same provisional-income formula used for SS). RetIQ applies this automatically — you do not need to configure anything differently from regular SS taxation.

Survivor Benefits When an SSDI Recipient Dies

If you're modeling survivor scenarios (in the Personal tab), SSDI interacts with survivor benefits in important ways:

  • SSDI stops at death. If the SSDI recipient passes away, their disability payments end immediately. The surviving spouse does not inherit the SSDI payment itself.
  • Survivor SS benefit from the deceased's record. The surviving spouse can claim a survivor benefit based on the deceased's Social Security work record. RetIQ uses the deceased's PIA to calculate this. If the deceased was on SSDI but had no separate SS income entered, RetIQ uses the SSDI monthly amount as the PIA (since SSDI is calculated from the same work record).
  • The higher-of rule. The surviving spouse receives the higher of their own SS benefit or the survivor benefit — not both. Before the survivor's own SS claim age, they receive just the survivor benefit as a bridge. At their own claim age, RetIQ takes the maximum of the two.
  • Survivor is also on SSDI. If the surviving spouse is also on SSDI, SSA pays the difference between the survivor benefit and the SSDI amount (if the survivor benefit is higher). RetIQ models this correctly — SSDI income continues, and any excess survivor benefit is added on top.

Common Scenarios

Scenario 1: Early disability, long SSDI period. Person becomes disabled at 45, starts SSDI at 45. Medicare kicks in at 47 (eliminating 18 years of pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs). SSDI converts to SS at FRA (67). Set Start Age = 45, End Age = 66. RetIQ automatically starts SS at age 67 regardless of what you set as the SS Claim Age.

Scenario 2: Late-career disability. Person becomes disabled at 58, starts SSDI at 58. Medicare kicks in at 60 (5 years earlier than normal). SSDI converts to SS at 67. Set Start Age = 58, End Age = 66. The engine overrides SS Claim Age to 67 (FRA).

Scenario 3: Spouse on SSDI with survivor planning. Primary earner is healthy; spouse is on SSDI. Configure SSDI for the spouse, then use the Survivor section in the Personal tab. If the spouse passes, their SSDI stops and the primary loses that household income. If the primary passes, the spouse on SSDI may be able to receive a survivor benefit on top of their SSDI (the difference, if the primary's record is larger).

Where to find your SSDI amount: Your benefit letter from SSA states your monthly disability amount. If you haven't applied yet, log in to my Social Security and check the disability estimate on your statement. This is an estimate based on your current earnings record.

10. Healthcare Tab

If you retire before Medicare eligibility, you need to bridge the healthcare gap:

  • Annual Premium: Estimated annual cost for ACA marketplace, COBRA, or private insurance.
  • Medical Inflation: Annual increase in healthcare costs. Default: 5% (healthcare historically inflates faster than general CPI).

The tab shows the cumulative healthcare cost during the gap years and the final-year premium to help you plan.

SSDI and early Medicare: If you have SSDI enabled, RetIQ automatically shortens the healthcare gap. SSDI recipients qualify for Medicare after 24 months of benefits, so your gap coverage ends at your SSDI Start Age + 2 (or 65, whichever is earlier). See the SSDI Tab section for details.

11. Medicare Tab

Medicare modeling begins at age 65 for most people, or earlier if SSDI triggers the 24-month early eligibility rule (see SSDI Tab). Key components:

  • Base Part B premium: $185/month (2025).
  • IRMAA surcharges: Additional premiums triggered when your MAGI (from 2 years prior) exceeds income thresholds. Roth conversions count toward MAGI and can trigger IRMAA.
  • Medical inflation: Medicare costs grow at 5% annually (configurable).

The Medicare tab shows a year-by-year breakdown of premiums, IRMAA surcharges, and total Medicare costs. This feeds into the projection as a healthcare expense line.

Note: Roth conversions increase your MAGI and can push you into higher IRMAA brackets. The Roth Conversion Optimizer accounts for this when scoring strategies.

12. Roth Conversion Tab

This tab has two sections that work together: the Strategy (your active plan) and the Optimizer (a research tool). Understanding the relationship between them is key.

Roth Conversion Strategy (your active plan)

Whatever you configure here is your live conversion plan — it feeds directly into the Dashboard, Projection table, Monte Carlo, and Scenarios. Toggle the checkbox to enable or disable conversions entirely.

Six strategy types:

  • Fixed Amount: Convert a set dollar amount each year (e.g., $50K/yr).
  • Fill Tax Bracket: Convert enough each year to fill taxable income up to a target bracket (e.g., fill the 22% bracket). This is the most common approach — it automatically accounts for Social Security, pensions, SSDI, earned income, and taxable withdrawals already filling the bracket, then converts only the remaining gap. As your income mix changes year to year (e.g., SS kicks in at 67), the conversion amount adjusts automatically.
  • Smart Fill (IRMAA-Aware): Same as Fill Tax Bracket but additionally caps the conversion before crossing the next IRMAA threshold. This prevents surprise Medicare surcharges that can cost $2,000–$8,000+/year. Recommended for anyone aged 63+ where IRMAA is a real concern (2-year lookback means conversions at 63 affect premiums at 65).
  • Target MAGI Ceiling: Convert up to a total MAGI limit. Useful for staying below IRMAA thresholds.
  • % of Pre-Tax Balance: Convert a percentage of remaining pre-tax balance each year. The amount naturally decreases as the balance shrinks.
  • Custom Schedule: Specify a different conversion amount for each year. You can edit amounts manually or click Generate Optimal to have RetIQ automatically compute the best amount for each year based on your complete tax landscape.

Start / End Age defines the conversion window. The sweet spot is typically between retirement (when earned income drops) and RMD start age (when forced distributions begin).

Impact Analysis

Directly below the strategy inputs, this section compares your current strategy against doing no conversions at all. Key metrics include total converted, tax cost, lifetime tax delta, RMD reduction, IRMAA impact, final net worth change, and Breakeven Age.

Breakeven Age is when your tax-adjusted net worth (portfolio value minus cumulative lifetime taxes paid) with conversions catches up to the no-conversion path. Because conversions pay taxes upfront, your wealth temporarily falls behind. The breakeven age tells you how long you must live for the strategy to pay off. Three outcomes are possible:

  • A specific age (e.g., 82) — the most common result. Before this age, conversion taxes are a net cost to your estate.
  • Immediate — conversions never fall behind, typically when amounts are small or the bracket is very low.
  • Never — the conversion cost is not recovered within the projection. Consider reducing amounts or shortening the window.

A Tax-Adjusted Net Worth chart shows both paths side by side so you can see the dip during conversion years and the crossover point visually. Below it, the existing Pre-Tax balance, Roth balance, and annual conversion charts provide further detail. A callout box explains the mortality trade-off: if you do not survive to the breakeven age, the upfront taxes represent real money lost.

This section updates instantly whenever you change any strategy parameter.

Roth Conversion Optimizer (research tool)

Click Find Optimal Strategy to test ~300+ combinations of amounts ($5K–$200K), bracket targets (fill 12%/22%/24% and their IRMAA-aware Smart Fill variants), and age windows. The optimizer ranks the top 8 results in a table with these columns:

  • Converted / Conv Tax: Total amount moved to Roth and the taxes paid on those conversions.
  • Tax / RMD / IRMAA vs Base: Lifetime savings (or costs) compared to doing no conversions.
  • Final NW vs Base: Difference in ending portfolio value.
  • Breakeven: The age at which tax-adjusted net worth catches up to the no-conversion path. “Immed.” means conversions never fall behind; a number means you need to survive to that age for the strategy to pay off; “Never” means the cost isn’t recovered.
  • Score (0–100): A weighted composite of tax savings, IRMAA reduction, and net worth gain. Higher is better. Use the radio buttons above the table to shift the weighting toward tax minimization, net worth maximization, or a balanced mix.

Below the table, the best strategy is expanded with a Tax-Adjusted Net Worth chart (showing the breakeven crossover), a Lifetime Tax Burden chart, and an Account Composition chart. A breakeven callout summarizes the mortality trade-off.

The optimizer does not change your plan automatically. It shows you what could work better and lets you decide.

Click Apply on any row to load that strategy into your active plan. The Strategy section updates with the new settings, the Impact Analysis re-computes, and all other tabs (Dashboard, Projection, Monte Carlo) reflect the change. The optimizer table stays visible with a “✓ Applied” marker so you can compare.

Generate Optimal Schedule (adaptive per-year optimization)

While the Optimizer tests uniform strategies (same rule every year), Generate Optimal creates a truly adaptive schedule — a different conversion amount for each year, tuned to that year’s specific tax landscape.

To use it: select Custom Schedule from the Strategy dropdown, then click Generate Optimal. RetIQ runs a greedy forward pass that, for each year in your conversion window, tests ~15 candidate amounts (including IRMAA-safe bracket fills and fixed amounts), evaluates the full lifetime impact of each, and picks the best one. A refinement pass then adjusts each year considering its neighbors.

The result populates the editable per-year table. You can review the amounts, tweak any year manually, and see the Impact Analysis update instantly. This is especially powerful for situations where:

  • Income changes mid-window: e.g., a gap between retirement and SS start creates “golden years” where aggressive conversion is optimal, then SS kicks in and the amount should drop.
  • IRMAA cliffs vary by year: the 2-year lookback means conversion decisions at different ages affect IRMAA at different future ages.
  • You want a personalized strategy: rather than a uniform rule, you want the exact dollar amount for each year of your retirement.
Performance note: Generate Optimal runs ~120 full projections (15 candidates × 8 years). This typically takes under 500ms. The quality depends on the scoring mode (Balanced, Tax Min, or NW Max) — the same radio buttons used by the Optimizer control the weighting.
Typical workflow: (1) Start with a simple strategy (Smart Fill 22% is a great default). (2) Review the Impact Analysis — check the breakeven age. (3) Run the Optimizer to see if a different window or bracket works better. (4) For maximum optimization, switch to Custom Schedule and click Generate Optimal — this often beats the best uniform strategy by reducing IRMAA and adapting to income changes. (5) Tweak individual years if needed. (6) Check the Dashboard and Monte Carlo to confirm the strategy works across market scenarios. Note: the Optimizer tests fixed-amount, fill-bracket, and smart-fill strategies. For Target MAGI, % of Balance, or custom fine-tuning, configure those manually.

13. Charity / QCD Tab

  • Annual Charitable Giving: Total planned charitable donations per year.
  • Use QCD: Enable Qualified Charitable Distributions from IRAs. QCDs satisfy RMDs without being included in taxable income. 2025 limit: $108,000/year.

If QCD is enabled, charitable giving is sourced from RMD amounts first, reducing taxable income. Any giving beyond RMDs is treated as a standard charitable deduction.

14. Legacy Tab

Set a target inheritance amount (today's dollars). The dashboard and projection track whether your plan achieves this goal at end-of-plan. The legacy target is inflation-adjusted in projections.

15. Liabilities Tab

Track debts that affect your annual expenses:

  • Name: Label (e.g., "Mortgage", "Car Loan", "Alimony").
  • Type: Category (Mortgage, Alimony, Loan, Other) — for labeling and organization.
  • Monthly Payment: Current payment amount.
  • Active Age Range: Start and end ages for the payment. For example, a mortgage from age 45 to 60 adds 15 years of payments to your expenses.

The annual total of all debt payments is shown and factored into the projection. When the active age range ends, the expense disappears from the projection.

17. Dashboard

The dashboard provides a high-level summary of your plan:

  • Key metrics: Final net worth, years funded, total taxes, total SS income, legacy achievement.
  • Alert cards: Warnings about portfolio depletion, high tax years, or RMD spikes.
  • Summary chart: Net worth trajectory with retirement age marker.

The dashboard updates instantly when any input changes.

18. Projection Table

The year-by-year projection table is the core output. Each row is one year of your plan. Columns include:

Age, Year, Earned Income, Social Security, SSDI, Pension, Expenses, Tax, RMD, Roth Conversion, Pre-Tax Balance, Roth Balance, Taxable Balance, Net Worth, Medicare, IRMAA, QCD, and more depending on what features are enabled. Columns with all-zero values are automatically hidden.

The header row is sticky so column labels remain visible while scrolling. A totals row at the bottom sums lifetime values. The table scrolls both horizontally and vertically.

Use the Today's $ / Future $ toggle in the header to view values in inflation-adjusted or nominal terms.

Tip: Use the CSV Export button (in the Projection view) to download the table for analysis in Excel or Google Sheets.

19. Monte Carlo Simulation

The Monte Carlo engine runs hundreds of randomized projections, varying both investment returns and inflation each year based on configurable standard deviations.

Configuration

  • Number of Simulations: Default 500. Configurable up to 2,000 for higher precision (slower).
  • Return Std Dev: Year-to-year volatility of investment returns. Default: 6%.
  • Inflation Std Dev: Year-to-year inflation volatility. Default: 1.5%.

Results

  • Success Rate: Percentage of simulations where money lasts through end-of-plan.
  • Percentile Chart: Shows 10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, and 90th percentile net worth trajectories.
  • Worst Case: The worst outcome across all simulations.
Interpretation: A 90%+ success rate is generally considered strong. Below 80% suggests your plan may need adjustment — reduce expenses, delay retirement, or increase savings.

20. Scenario Comparison

Test how changes to key assumptions affect your plan. RetIQ includes 18 built-in scenarios:

Single-variable: Retire at 60, Retire at 67, Higher expenses (+20%), Lower return (5%), Higher inflation (5%), SS at 62, SS at 70, and more.

Combinations: "Low Return + High Inflation," "Early Retire + Lower Return," "SS at 70 + Roth Conv," "+20% Expenses + High Inflation."

Results are shown as a comparison table and multi-category chart. The selected scenario categories (SS Income, Net Worth, Tax, etc.) can be toggled to compare any metric across all scenarios simultaneously.

21. Save & Load

Auto-Save

RetIQ automatically saves your plan to the browser's local storage after every change. If you close and reopen the app, your data is still there.

HTML Snapshot

Click "HTML" in the header to export a self-contained HTML file of your entire plan. This file includes all inputs, charts, and projections and can be opened in any browser without the app. Great for archiving, printing, or sharing with an advisor.

Load

Click "Load" to import a previously saved JSON or HTML snapshot file. This replaces your current plan data.

CSV Export

In the Projection view, use the CSV export button to download the projection table for analysis in Excel or Google Sheets.

Important: Browser local storage is per-device. To transfer your plan to another device, export an HTML or JSON file and import it on the other device.

22. Install & Licensing

Installing the PWA

Click the "Install" button in the header (when available) to add RetIQ to your home screen or desktop. The installed app works offline and launches like a native application.

Free vs. Pro

The free demo lets you explore every feature with sample data. Pro ($29 launch price, $49 after launch) unlocks full customization, data saving, and exports. Your license is tied to your email and can be activated on multiple devices.

Activating a License

After purchase, click "Activate" in the header and enter your license key (sent to your email). The app verifies the key and unlocks Pro features. License verification requires an internet connection the first time; after that, the status is cached locally.

23. FAQ

Is my data secure?

Yes. RetIQ runs entirely in your browser. No financial data is ever sent to any server. The only network calls are for license verification (your email and key only) and optional PWA updates.

Which tax brackets does RetIQ use?

2025 federal tax brackets for both Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) and Single filers. The filing status switches automatically to Single after a spouse passes in survivor scenarios. State tax uses a simplified effective rate based on the state you select from the dropdown in the Assumptions tab (all 50 states and DC are available). The brackets are applied to computed taxable income each year.

How accurate are the Social Security estimates?

RetIQ matches SSA calculations exactly when using the manual entry mode (entering your FRA benefit from ssa.gov). The income-based estimate uses 2025 bend points and is a good approximation but may differ from SSA's indexed earnings calculation.

Does RetIQ account for Required Minimum Distributions?

Yes. RMDs are computed per the Uniform Lifetime Table with SECURE Act 2.0 start ages. They are sourced from pre-tax accounts and included in taxable income. QCDs can offset RMDs if enabled.

Can I model my spouse dying before me?

Yes. Set "Spouse Death Age" in the Personal tab. The projection will switch to single filing status, adjust expenses by the survivor ratio, and calculate survivor SS benefits from that age forward.

What does the Monte Carlo success rate mean?

It is the percentage of randomized simulations where your money lasts through end-of-plan. A 95% success rate means that in 950 out of 1,000 random market scenarios, your plan survived. Generally, 90%+ is considered strong.

Can I use RetIQ on my phone?

Yes. RetIQ is fully responsive. Install it as a PWA on iOS or Android for the best mobile experience.

How do I reset all my data?

Clear your browser's local storage for retiqstudio.com, or use your browser's developer tools (Application > Local Storage > Clear). A fresh reload will start with default values.

Does SSDI affect my Medicare start age?

Yes. RetIQ automatically starts Medicare at SSDI Start Age + 2 years (or 65, whichever is first). This reflects the federal 24-month qualifying period. Pre-Medicare healthcare gap costs, Medicare premiums, and IRMAA all adjust accordingly. See the SSDI Tab section for full details and scenarios.

What happens to my spouse's benefits if I'm on SSDI and die?

Your SSDI payments stop, but your surviving spouse can claim a survivor benefit based on your Social Security work record. RetIQ calculates this from your PIA (or your SSDI amount if no separate SS income was entered). The survivor receives the higher of their own SS benefit or the survivor benefit — not both.
